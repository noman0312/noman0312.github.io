Current Project

UNIGRID sodium-ion BESS integration and grid-interface development

This project focuses on the integration, assembly, and validation of two 150 kWh cabinet-style sodium-ion battery energy storage systems at Khalifa University, aiming to deliver field-ready prototypes for validation with ACWA Power.

2 cabinet-style sodium-ion BESS prototypes for integration and validation
150 kWh target energy capacity for each cabinet-scale storage unit
100 kW minimum continuous bidirectional inverter target per unit at 50°C ambient
Current active engineering work on integration planning, component selection, control, and validation flow
Project Overview

Cabinet-scale sodium-ion storage for future grid applications

Khalifa University is acting as the integration hub for this project, converting UNIGRID's cell and pack technology into complete BESS cabinets ready for field validation. The work spans detailed engineering, enclosure integration, power-conversion selection, control-system implementation, communication design, safety validation, and final testing.

The project is structured around building two field-ready prototypes that can move from engineering design into practical cabinet assembly, staged power-up, charge-discharge verification, grid tests, and documented handover.

Architecture

System design and integration workflow

The project combines battery-pack integration, power conversion, automation, communications, and field-oriented safety engineering.

Technical Targets

Key engineering specifications

  • Two cabinet-style 150 kWh BESS prototypes based on sodium-ion battery packs.
  • Bidirectional grid-tie inverter target of at least 100 kW continuous per unit at 50°C ambient.
  • Nominal DC operating range around 480-850 Vdc with 400 Vac three-phase plus neutral output.
  • Communication support around Modbus TCP, CAN bus, and utility-ready communication capability.
  • Grid-forming capability and ancillary-service readiness included in the inverter requirements.
  • Outdoor cabinet requirements including IP55-class protection, thermal management, and integrated safety peripherals.
Integration Scope

What the project requires in practice

  • Detailed engineering review, commercial component selection, and final BOM generation for procurement.
  • Mechanical cabinet assembly with battery packs, inverter, DC protection hardware, HVAC, and control-panel integration.
  • Electrical system integration including DC architecture, schematics, contactors, fuses, breakers, grounding, and insulation checks.
  • Controller, HMI, and communication implementation for idle, charging, discharging, and fault states.
  • Safety integration, staged power-up, low-power checks, full-power testing, and final test reporting before field handover.
Design Challenges

Problems I am actively solving in the UNIGRID project

This project is not only about assembling a cabinet. It is about resolving the open engineering questions that determine whether a battery system becomes field-ready and utility-relevant.

Problem 1: the inverter and protection design depend on battery data

Before a cabinet architecture can be frozen, the electrical behavior of the sodium-ion packs must be understood in enough detail to size the PCS, define the protection hardware, and confirm the operating limits seen by the converter.

  • I am using the engineering review stage to resolve the DC voltage window, current ratings, ripple-current tolerance, internal resistance, and fault-current behavior of the battery packs.
  • Those answers determine inverter sizing, whether a DC-DC stage is necessary, and how the protection and grounding strategy should be designed.
  • This is one of the areas where I am leading the power-electronics and grid-interface decisions for the project.

Problem 2: cabinet integration must survive hot outdoor operation

The project targets outdoor cabinet operation with high ambient temperature, which means thermal behavior, airflow, cabinet insulation, sensor placement, and abnormal-event response all have to be treated as first-order engineering requirements.

  • I am contributing to the thermal-management and safety architecture by linking battery operating limits with HVAC, internal circulation, and cabinet-level safety requirements.
  • The design includes smoke or off-gas detection, E-stop behavior, alarm integration, and fire-suppression decisions that must align with the battery chemistry and the test environment.
  • Rather than leaving safety as a checklist item, the integration plan treats it as part of the core system design.

Problem 3: multi-vendor components have to operate as one system

A cabinet BESS only works when the battery packs, BMS, inverter, controller, HMI, and monitoring system communicate reliably and follow a clear operating sequence. That coordination layer is often where integration projects become delayed or unstable.

  • I am involved in the control and communication framework that manages idle, charge, discharge, and fault states.
  • The work includes Modbus and CAN-based integration, local HMI behavior, data logging, and the translation of high-level operating commands into PCS actions.
  • My role connects converter-level control thinking with full-system automation and grid-interface behavior.

Problem 4: the prototypes must be commissioned safely and systematically

Projects like this fail if commissioning is improvised. The handover only becomes credible when the build is followed by disciplined verification, staged energization, and documented testing.

  • The workflow includes unpowered continuity and insulation checks before first energization.
  • The power-up path then moves through staged commissioning, low-power charge-discharge verification, and full-power grid-connected tests.
  • I am helping define the validation path so the final units are not only assembled, but documented and defensible for external testing.
Leadership Contribution

Where I am leading the work

My contribution is centered on the power-conversion and system-integration side of the project. That includes inverter and DC-side selection strategy, protection coordination, electrical architecture, grounding, grid-interface requirements, and the control framework needed to make the BESS operate safely and automatically.

I am also directly involved in the control-system and data-acquisition layer, including communication protocols, HMI and local-control logic, and the testing flow needed to move the prototype from engineering planning to validated operation. In practice, that means my role spans both converter-level engineering and the broader system behavior expected from a modern BESS in a future converter-dominated power grid.

Current Engineering Questions

Important design and validation issues

  • Battery voltage window, charge-discharge profiles, ripple-current tolerance, and fault-current limits.
  • Thermal behavior, airflow needs, hot-spot locations, and operating limits under elevated ambient temperature.
  • Cabinet-level safety design, including smoke or off-gas detection, venting behavior, and fire suppression choice.
  • System hazards related to BMS, controller, protection, and power-electronics failures.
  • Definition of automation flow, charge-discharge test cycles, and full-capacity verification strategy.
  • Procurement timing and BOM closure, which directly affect the project schedule and build readiness.
